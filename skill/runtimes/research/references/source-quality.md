# Research Source Quality

Read this reference when selecting sources or reconciling conflicting evidence.

## Source Priority

Prefer sources in this order when they directly support the claim:

1. Laws, regulators, standards bodies, and official datasets.
2. Direct product documentation, pricing, terms, and release notes.
3. Original research, filings, and first-party measurements.
4. Independent industry analysis with visible methodology.
5. Reputable reporting that cites primary evidence.
6. Community reports and user discussions for qualitative signals.

Search-result snippets are discovery aids, not final evidence.

## Evaluation Criteria

For every material source, consider:

- Authority: who produced it and why?
- Directness: does it support this exact claim?
- Recency: is the publication and underlying data current enough?
- Method: can the collection or calculation be inspected?
- Incentive: is the source selling or defending something?
- Geography and segment: does the evidence apply to the target context?

## Contradictions

When sources disagree:

1. Check whether they measure different populations, dates, or definitions.
2. Prefer transparent methods over confident summaries.
3. Report the disagreement when it affects the decision.
4. Lower confidence instead of selecting the most convenient number.

## Citation Rule

Place a direct source link near each material externally verifiable claim. Include an access date in the source list when information is likely to change.
